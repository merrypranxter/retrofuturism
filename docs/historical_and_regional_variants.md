# Historical Waves and Regional Variants

## Major historical waves

1. **Proto-industrial speculation (1860s-1910s)**
   - Steam and empire infrastructure imagined as world-changing technology.
2. **Machine-age mass future (1920s-1940s)**
   - Streamline modernity and ideological infrastructure.
3. **Postwar domestic-space optimism (1950s-1960s)**
   - Consumer futurity + household automation + early space branding.
4. **Programmatic aerospace realism (1960s-1970s)**
   - Mission-control and state-science visual language.
5. **Analog systems futurism (1970s-1990s)**
   - Interface density, industrial modularity, human-in-the-loop operations.
6. **Early internet future optimism (1995-2005)**
   - Glossy, connected, lifestyle-forward digital imagination.

## Regional specificity guide

### US lineage
- Strong coupling of consumer products and futurist branding.
- Clear progression from Googie/atompunk to Y2K techno-pop marketing.

### Soviet/Eastern Bloc lineage
- Monumental civic framing, collectivist messaging, and science as ideology.
- More infrastructural and educational than lifestyle-centric.

### Japanese lineage
- Mecha industrial detailing + dense urban infrastructural storytelling.
- Strong crossover between animation grammar and hard-surface design.

### Cross-diasporic speculative lineage
- Future as cultural reclamation and continuity, not merely tech novelty.
- Symbol systems often carry memory, ritual, and political context.

## Variant documentation standard

When documenting a regional variant, include:

- Historical driver (policy, industry, media)
- Dominant materials and production constraints
- Distinct symbolic vocabulary
- Common narrative subjects
- How the variant differs from globalized "default retrofuture"
